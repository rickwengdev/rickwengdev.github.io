<p>Most people think Buy, Borrow, Die is a tax trick for the ultra-wealthy.</p>
<p>After months of research, I found the real value is not tax at all.</p>
<p>It is <strong>capital efficiency</strong>.</p>

<hr />

<h3>Starting Point</h3>
<p>It started with a video.</p>
<p>The video explained that the wealthy rarely sell assets. Instead, they pledge stocks as collateral to borrow cash, deferring capital gains tax while letting assets keep compounding.</p>
<p>Most people concluded:</p>
<blockquote><p>"Rich people are rich. This doesn't work for normal people."</p></blockquote>
<p>My first reaction was not envy. It was:</p>
<p><strong>Why?</strong></p>
<p>If someone can do it, it is not magic. It is a mechanism you can understand.</p>

<hr />

<h3>Strip Away the Tax Halo First</h3>
<p>Here is something many articles never say.</p>
<p>The tax advantages of Buy, Borrow, Die are <strong>American</strong>.</p>
<p>Deferred capital gains, step-up basis at death — these only matter for U.S. tax residents. If you are a non-U.S. tax resident, selling U.S. stocks is often not subject to U.S. capital gains tax anyway. The "Die" step does not exist for you.</p>
<p>So is the model useless for everyone else?</p>
<p>Exactly the opposite.</p>
<p>Because the real core is a fact that holds across jurisdictions:</p>
<blockquote><p>Borrowing is not a taxable event.</p></blockquote>
<p>Wherever you are, selling may trigger tax. Borrowing does not.</p>
<p>Tax advantage is American.</p>
<p>Capital efficiency is universal.</p>

<hr />

<h3>How I Got to This Model</h3>
<p>I did not see this clearly from the start.</p>
<p>At first, I thought Buy, Borrow, Die was mostly about "Borrow" — just control LTV and you are fine, right?</p>
<p>That idea was overturned five times over the next few months.</p>

<h4>Turning Point 1: LTV Is Not a Fixed Number — It Is Market State</h4>
<p>My earliest idea: set a safe LTV and the system can run long-term.</p>
<p>Once I started simulating bear markets, I realized the same LTV means completely different risk in different environments.</p>
<p>Leverage that looks safe in a bull market is not the same in a bear market.</p>
<p>LTV is not a fixed number. It is a control variable that must adjust with market state.</p>

<h4>Turning Point 2: The Real Risk Is Not Return — It Is Liquidity</h4>
<p>Then I started studying historical bear markets.</p>
<p>Most people do not fail because of bad investment decisions.</p>
<p>They fail because they run out of liquidity — forced to sell assets they intended to hold long-term at the worst possible time.</p>
<p>So I reframed the question: if the market crashes, can I avoid selling assets?</p>
<p>That is when I started researching SGOV, IB01, and cash buffers.</p>

<h4>Turning Point 3: Borrowing Is Not the Core — Cash Flow Is</h4>
<p>Most Buy, Borrow, Die discussions focus on borrowing.</p>
<p>But the more I studied, the clearer it became: what actually supports the whole system is cash flow.</p>
<p>With stable income, you can actively repay and de-lever when borrowing efficiency drops — instead of being forced by the market.</p>
<p>Borrowing is just a tool.</p>
<p>Cash flow is the stabilizer.</p>

<h4>Turning Point 4: Different Infrastructure, Different Risk Models</h4>
<p>Then I started comparing brokerages, CeFi, and on-chain lending.</p>
<p>I originally thought they were just different borrowing sources.</p>
<p>Research showed that the same 30% LTV represents completely different safety margins on different platforms.</p>
<p>From that point on, I stopped asking "what is the optimal LTV?"</p>
<p>I started asking: how does this infrastructure die?</p>

<h4>Turning Point 5: I Was Not Actually Studying Buy, Borrow, Die</h4>
<p>Along the way, I realized BBD was just the entry point.</p>
<p>The question I was really trying to answer:</p>
<blockquote><p>How do you keep quality assets in the market as long as possible while meeting life's liquidity needs?</p></blockquote>
<p>At that point I realized I was not building a borrowing strategy.</p>
<p>I was building a <strong>Capital Efficiency</strong> model.</p>

<hr />

<h3>The Current Model</h3>
<p>Many people assume this encourages high leverage.</p>
<p>It is the opposite.</p>
<ol>
  <li>Build a core portfolio of long-term holdings.</li>
  <li>Keep DCA-ing consistently.</li>
  <li>Maintain stable external income.</li>
  <li>Fund living expenses primarily through collateralized borrowing, not asset sales.</li>
  <li>Control risk — do not chase maximum leverage.</li>
</ol>
<p>Borrowing is not the goal. It is a liquidity management tool.</p>

<hr />

<h3>Can Ordinary People Actually Replicate This? Here Are the Tools</h3>
<p>The most common objection is access.</p>
<p>So let's look at access directly.</p>

<h4>Traditional Brokerages</h4>
<ul>
  <li><strong>IBKR Portfolio Margin:</strong> $110,000 account minimum, relatively low rates.</li>
  <li><strong>IBKR / Fidelity / Schwab standard margin:</strong> Much lower minimums, but worse borrowing terms and ratios.</li>
  <li><strong>Schwab Pledged Asset Line, Fidelity equivalents:</strong> Typically require six-figure assets.</li>
</ul>

<h4>CeFi</h4>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Platforms like Nexo:</strong> No asset minimum, but you hand custody to a counterparty. Celsius and BlockFi users can tell you what that means.</li>
</ul>

<h4>On-Chain</h4>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Protocols like Morpho and Vesu:</strong> Zero minimum. Even $500 in assets can be pledged for a loan. Rules are in the contract — no one approves you.</li>
</ul>

<p>So "ordinary people can't do this" is no longer true in 2026.</p>
<p>The barrier is not whether you can borrow.</p>
<p>The barrier is whether you understand what you are borrowing against.</p>

<hr />

<h3>A Concrete Example</h3>
<p>Assume:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Assets: $10,000 in quality long-term positions.</li>
  <li>Loan: $1,500 (15% LTV).</li>
  <li>Rate: 8%.</li>
</ul>
<p>Annual interest cost: $120.</p>
<p>What you get: $10,000 stays fully invested. If long-term annual return is 7%, expected value is $700 per year.</p>
<p>Liquidation risk? If the liquidation line is 80% LTV, the asset would need to fall more than 80% before this position is liquidated.</p>
<p>This is not leverage gambling.</p>
<p>This is paying $120 a year for the option not to sell.</p>
<p>Of course, if you push LTV to 50%, the story changes completely.</p>
<p>The model did not change.</p>
<p>You did.</p>

<hr />

<h3>Two Ways to Die</h3>
<p>I do not start with returns. I start with death.</p>
<p>Under what conditions does this system die?</p>

<h4>Traditional Brokerage Death: Margin Call</h4>
<p>The broker notifies you to add collateral. There is usually buffer time. You can react — sell a portion, add cash, reduce leverage.</p>
<p>You die slowly, but you die from hesitation.</p>

<h4>On-Chain Death: Automatic Liquidation</h4>
<p>No phone call. No notice. No grace period.</p>
<p>The contract runs 24/7. The moment price hits the line, the liquidation bot executes. A 3 AM crash will not wait for you to wake up.</p>
<p>You die fast, and you die from your LTV setting.</p>

<p>Same model. Two infrastructures. Risk management must be designed separately.</p>

<hr />

<h3>How I Reduce Risk</h3>

<h4>Control LTV</h4>
<p>A bull market does not mean you can keep raising borrowing indefinitely.</p>
<p>The key question: if the market crashes suddenly, do I still have time to adjust — or am I forced to liquidate?</p>
<p>On-chain users should use more conservative LTV than brokerage users, because there is no buffer time.</p>

<h4>Keep Liquidity Reserves</h4>
<p>Beyond core assets, keep a portion in cash or cash-like instruments:</p>
<ul>
  <li>SGOV, IB01</li>
  <li>Stablecoins (where you can access them immediately)</li>
  <li>Cash</li>
</ul>
<p>They are not for return.</p>
<p>They are for survival.</p>

<h4>Interest Rate Is a Dynamic Judgment, Not a Fixed Threshold</h4>
<p>Whether borrowing cost is reasonable depends on your long-term asset expectations and whether cash flow can support it.</p>
<p>If borrowing efficiency drops:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Reduce DCA.</li>
  <li>Repay with cash flow.</li>
  <li>Lower borrowing ratio.</li>
</ul>
<p>The point is not to insist on borrowing.</p>
<p>It is to keep the overall system efficient.</p>

<hr />

<h3>This Is Not a Belief System</h3>
<p>No model should be a religion.</p>
<p>If a tool works, use it. If it fails, adjust.</p>
<p>I will keep looking for holes.</p>
<p>If someone presents a better counterexample, I welcome it — it means the model can still improve.</p>
<p>What I actually believe in three sentences:</p>
<blockquote>
  <p>Cash is for spending.</p>
  <p>Assets are for holding.</p>
  <p>Borrowing is for managing liquidity.</p>
</blockquote>
